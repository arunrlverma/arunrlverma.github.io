Why I built this

I built this because my own cancer file had signals I didn't want to miss.

I found that my cancer overexpresses DLL3 — immunosuppressive, but not featureless. That changed my question from "what does the report say?" to "what might we be missing?" — signals I took to my oncologists to ask about, not conclusions I acted on alone.

Built for that moment, by someone who needed it himself. One person's experience; the analysis doesn't promise actionable findings for anyone.

Under the hood

Not a chatbot. A genomics pipeline with an AI reasoning layer.

Your data runs through the same open tools cancer researchers use, on GRCh38. An AI layer then weighs the evidence and writes it in plain language — every finding tied to its source.

Variant calling

Somatic mutations from the consensus of two callers — Mutect2 and MuSE — annotated with VEP. Not one tool's output.

Expression & fusions

STAR, Salmon & Arriba — what your tumor expresses, and the fusions driving it.

Copy number

PureCN & FACETS — amplifications, deletions, purity, and ploidy.

Immune & neoantigens

OptiType & MHCflurry — HLA typing and what the immune system might see.

Drugs & dependencies

Trial and drug databases, plus DepMap — hypotheses from features shared with model systems, never a prediction of your response.

AI synthesis

Evidence-weighted and source-cited — every claim tied to a trial, database entry, or paper.

Questions

Before you upload.

Straight answers. If something isn't here, email me directly.

Is this medical advice?

No — it's an educational and research tool, not a medical device, diagnosis, or treatment plan. Review every finding with your oncology team before you act on it.

Is this just ChatGPT in a wrapper?

No. Your files run through a real genomics pipeline — the same open tools used in cancer research (Mutect2, MuSE, VEP, STAR, MHCflurry, DepMap), with mutations called from the consensus of two tools. AI is the layer that weighs the evidence and writes it in plain language, with every claim tied to its source.

Where does my genomic data go?

It's analyzed in our own pipeline — not handed to a third party to do the genomics. It's never sold, shared, or used to train AI, and you can delete it permanently at any time.

Could it reveal inherited (germline) risk?

If you include matched normal-tissue data, the analysis can surface inherited (germline) signals that may affect blood relatives — and, rarely, unexpected family relationships. It isn't designed to diagnose inherited conditions; confirm any inherited finding with a genetic counselor.

What do I need to upload?

Existing tumor sequencing — ideally with matched normal data — plus any lab or pathology reports. Command Center analyzes data you already have; it doesn't generate new sequencing. Most hospitals release your files on request, and more input means a deeper analysis.

How long does it take?

The pipeline runs automatically once your files are uploaded. Most reports are ready within a few days; we email you when yours is done.

What if it doesn't find much?

Some cases carry fewer signals than others. The report still organizes what's there, shows what was checked, and flags the gaps — no finding is ever guaranteed, and that honesty is the point.

What about refunds?

Refunded in full if your files can't be processed. Once a report is successfully generated the fee is non-refundable, since the analysis has run — which is why the complete sample report is here to read first.
