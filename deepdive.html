How I think about treatment

IDH-mutant astrocytoma: targeted IDH inhibition plus exploratory personalized neoantigen vaccination

What I am trying to do is go after the tumor in two different ways: use an FDA-approved IDH inhibitor for the mutation that defines my cancer, and explore a personalized vaccine that tries to make selected tumor mutations visible to my immune system. This is my treatment story, not proof that the combination improves outcomes.

01

Vorasidenib

My tumor has IDH1 R132C. That is why IDH inhibition matters here. Vorasidenib is FDA-approved for grade 2 astrocytoma or oligodendroglioma with a susceptible IDH1 or IDH2 mutation after surgery, and it is the IDH inhibitor I am on now.

02

Personal neoantigen vaccine

The vaccine is exploratory and personal to my tumor. It uses mutation-spanning peptides, with the goal of helping T cells notice pieces of the cancer that should not exist in normal tissue.

Caveats & How to Read This

I generated a broader Triangle report from my data. It is useful, but it should be read as a synthesis layer: a way to organize questions and conversations, not a diagnosis or treatment plan.

Download the generated Triangle report
01

This is a synthesis, not a diagnosis

This report combines genomic, transcriptomic, and database evidence into one view. It is meant to support conversations with clinicians, not replace clinical judgment or established care pathways.

02

“Driver” does not always mean clinically actionable

Some genes are labeled as drivers based on computational or pan-cancer evidence. IDH1, ATRX, and TP53 are well-established for this tumor type; others are algorithmic candidates or context-dependent clues.

03

Pathogenic variants are not always cancer-relevant

Labels like “pathogenic” may come from databases such as ClinVar and can reflect inherited disease associations. That does not automatically mean the variant is driving this tumor.

04

Drug rankings are hypothesis-generating

The drug list matches tumor features to known targets and databases. “FDA approved” means approved somewhere, not necessarily for this disease, and many entries are not standard or appropriate in glioma.

05

Pathway matches are maps, not diagnoses

Pathways labeled as breast cancer, gastric cancer, or other diseases reflect shared signaling biology across cancers. They are reference maps, not statements that those diseases are present.

06

MGMT, TMB, and MSI need proper clinical assays

MGMT promoter methylation cannot be determined from RNA alone. TMB here is WES-derived and not calibrated for immunotherapy decisions. MSI is shown as a signal, not a clinical eligibility call.

07

Data quality and sampling matter

Tumor purity around 32% can reduce sensitivity. Some variants may be missed or under-called, and not every finding is confirmed across multiple pipelines. Absence of a signal is not proof of absence.

08

Experimental ideas remain investigational

Neoantigen vaccines, immune-targeting strategies, and off-label combinations are active research areas, not established standard of care. I treat them as exploratory.

09

This reflects one point in time

Tumors evolve. This report reflects a specific sample, a specific moment, and a specific interpretation layer. Future scans, treatments, or sequencing may change the picture.
